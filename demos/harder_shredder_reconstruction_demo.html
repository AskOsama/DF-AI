Now the page is shredded both ways — vertical and horizontal cuts — into a grid of tiles. Each tile must match its neighbours on two edges (left + top). Greedy fills the grid from a known top-left corner, picking the lowest-cost tile for each cell. Crank up the grid and watch what happens: comparisons grow like N², accuracy collapses, and the true search space (N!) explodes far beyond anything brute force could ever check.

Greedy raster-fill from a given top-left tile (an in-browser stand-in for proper cross-cut solvers). Real public-domain page (govdocs1 Federal Register). The lesson is scalability: greedy is O(N²) comparisons (tractable) but its quality does not scale — on a fine grid it falls apart. Use Instant solve to compare sizes fast.
